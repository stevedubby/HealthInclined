## Hook
You’ve been sitting for a while, then your foot or hand feels weird. It’s not quite pain—more like tingling or “pins and needles.” Then it eases once you move.

## Simple Explanation
Pins and needles often happen when a nerve gets temporarily “pressed” or irritated by your position. Sitting can also reduce movement in the joints and soft tissues, which makes nerves less comfortable briefly.

## Common Causes
- Sitting with legs crossed or leaning on one arm
- Slouching or holding the same posture for a long time
- Tight muscles that sit near a nerve path
- Overuse earlier in the day (desk work + repetitive movement)
- Dehydration and fatigue can make your body feel more sensitive

## When It’s Normal vs When to Pay Attention
### Normal (usually)
- Tingling starts after a specific position
- Symptoms improve within a few minutes after changing position
- No lasting numbness or weakness

### Pay attention
- Numbness or weakness lasts longer than expected
- You notice trouble moving a body part
- Symptoms keep returning in the same spot with no clear trigger
- Tingling spreads and becomes persistent
- You have significant pain or recent injury

## What You Can Do
- Change position often: stand up every 30–45 minutes
- Avoid leaning on elbows or crossing legs for long periods
- Gentle stretches: hip stretch, calf stretch, and shoulder rolls
- If it’s from desk posture, adjust chair/keyboard height and use arm support
- Hydrate and prioritize sleep so your body recovers

## Quick Summary
Pins and needles after sitting is commonly a temporary nerve compression from posture. Change positions, take short movement breaks, and do gentle stretches. If tingling lasts, comes with weakness, or becomes persistent, consider getting checked.
